TFCC recovery guide

Recovering from pinky-side wrist pain as a climber

TFCC injuries can make gripping, twisting, pushing, and climbing feel uncertain. This guide organizes recovery into simple stages: calm it down, restore motion, rebuild strength, and return to climbing carefully.

Important safety note

This website is for education only. TFCC injuries vary in severity. See a doctor, hand therapist, physical therapist, or occupational therapist if symptoms followed trauma, if pain is severe or worsening, if the wrist feels unstable, or if normal activity is not improving.

What is the TFCC?

The triangular fibrocartilage complex sits on the ulnar, or pinky, side of the wrist. It helps stabilize the wrist and forearm when the hand grips, rotates, or bears weight. Climbers may irritate this area through compression moves, mantles, slopers, underclings, hard pinches, falls, or repeated loaded wrist rotation.

1

Calm symptoms down

Reduce painful loading. Avoid sharp pain from climbing, push-ups, planks, yoga arm balances, heavy gripping, or forceful twisting. A brace, tape, or wrist widget-style support may help during daily tasks.

2

Restore comfortable motion

Begin gentle wrist and forearm motion only within a tolerable range. Do not stretch aggressively into sharp pain.

  • Wrist flexion and extension
  • Forearm pronation and supination
  • Radial and ulnar deviation, small range first
3

Rebuild strength

Progress to light isometrics and then gentle resistance. Strength work should feel controlled and should not create a next-day flare.

  • Isometric wrist extension and flexion
  • Isometric pronation and supination
  • Light band or hammer rotations
  • Grip work only when pain-free
4

Return to climbing gradually

Start with lower-intensity climbing and avoid positions that load the ulnar wrist. Reintroduce stressors one at a time so you know what your wrist tolerates.

  • Prefer jugs, open-hand positions, and controlled footwork
  • Avoid hard slopers, mantles, underclings, compression, and dynamic catches early
  • Stop if sharp pain, instability, or next-day worsening appears
